Dr. Sears

Dr. Darrel Sears

Associate Professor of Bible and Ministry

Ecclesiastes is a hard book to read and one I think many Christians avoid. The writing is often misunderstood as a book about life being meaningless. This is because of the way the Hebrew word "hevel" is interpreted in many English translations. The teacher says in Ecclesiastes 1:2, "Hevel! Hevel! Everything is utterly hevel!" It seems as if the teacher is saying there is no meaning to life, but actually, I think he is saying quite the opposite. Hevel can be translated as vapor or smoke, something that is hard to hold onto or something that vanishes quickly. I much prefer this translation and meaning. There's a great book by David Gibson called Living Life Backwards. He writes, "Ecclesiastes teaches us to live life backward. It encourages us to take the one thing in the future that is certain - our death - and work backward from that point into all the details and decisions and heartaches of our lives, and to think about them from the perspective of the end. It is the destination that makes sense of teh journey... I want to persuade you that only if you prepare to die can you really learn how to live (pg. 12)." If Gibson is correct, which I think he is, then Ecclesiastes isn't about the meaningless of life, but rather how we are to make meaning out of our vapor-like life.

Dr. Case

Dr. Michael L. Case

Professor Emiritus

The Preacher confies his obervations about life under the sun to what he can find out by wisdom (1.13), or what can be learned through reason and experience. He sees much that is "crooked" (1.15; 7.13). This includes a time for every experience, including painful ones (3.2-8). He grieves for the oppressed and laments evil deeds (4.1-3). In the end there is no theodicy or defense of God in the face of these crooked experiences. He notes that the unborn or dead are better off than the living because they do not have to live in such an uncertain world (4.2-3; 7.1-2). He offers no sustained discussion as to why these things happen. He knows God is in control, and will hold all to account for how they live (8.10-13; 11.9-10), but does not know why God has created the world as it is. In the end, we live in an uncertain and confusing world, but God is absolutely real. We should be grateful for the simple joys and know the days of darkness will be many (11.8). And "remember the Creator" firs, last and always (12.1-8).

Mark Miller

Mark C. Miller

Chair of Mathematics Department

In the book of Ecclesiastes, the Teacher confronts the reality of human suffering with refreshing honesty, dispelling the convenient notin that good deeds guarantee a prosperus, pain-free life. He characterizes human existence "under the sun" (1:14) as "meaningless" or vanity (1:2), the Hebrew word for which more literally points to an ephemeral vapor or smoke that is impossible to control or contain.

This perspective considers adversity not strictly as divine punishment--which at times can be the case--but also as a universal life occurance. The Teacher names a number of outright injustices before landing on the plain but unconfortable truth that "there area wicked men who get what the actionf of the righteous deserve" (8:14). The concusion that "time and chance happen to them all" (9:11), besides appealing to my mathematical instincts, strips away our illusion of control, forcing us to acknowledge the inherent unfairness and unpredictability of this fallen world.

In the shadow of inevitable adversity and the great equalizer of death (3:19–20), the Teacher concludes that enduring suffering requires a posture of reverent humility, concluding that the whole duty of humanity is to "fear God and keep his commandments" (12:13).
