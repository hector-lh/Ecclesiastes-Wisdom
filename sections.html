  • The Search of Meaning

    First, he looks for the value in wisdo, and he puts his "mind to study and to explore by wisdom all that is done under the heavens" (Ecclesiastes 1:13). However, he soon realizes that this is also meaningless and taht "the more knowledge, the more grief" (Ecclesiastes 1:18). After this, he forgets about wisdom and tries to find the meaning of life by indulging in "worldly pleasures" (The Book of Ecclesiastes Summary by Chapter 1:12). He concludes that folly is meaningles. Despite wisdom and folly being both meaningless and lead to the same fate, the teacher says "wisdom is better than folly" because it takes people out of darkness (Ecclesiastes 2:13).

  • Wisdom and Suffering

    The teacher says that he has seen the opression under the sun and that the people that are dead "are happier than the living," (Ecclesiastes 4:2), but even better are those who "have never been born," because they have not "seen the evil that is done under the sun" (Ecclesiastes 4:3). This made me think of the phrase by a famous German philosopher, Friedrich Nietzsche (as cited in Kasi, 2024) "To live is to suffer, to survive is to find some meaning in the suffering." As the previous section mentioned, there is a time for everything, including suffering, but how we deal with that is what matters. This is connected to wisdom, which is also talked about in this section. The teacher says that wisdom is like a "shelter," that "preserves those who have it" (Ecclesiastes 7:12). To live is not the same as just existing; we must learn to find meaning form our suffering, but there is no "one-size-fits-all solution" to that problem (Kasi). The teacher, for example, finds his meaning in fearing God, and by "enjoying the few days of life God has given" (Ecclesiastes 5:18).
