Main Sections

  • Introduction

    The author mentions that he is writing the words of "the teacher, son of David, King in Jerusalem" (Ecclesiastes 1:1). Scholars debate whether the teacher is Solomon or someone after him; whoever it is, "the wisdom it portrays comes from the 'one Shepherd' (12:11), the Lord himself" (Inreoduction to Ecclesiastes). The teacher asks what the meaning of life is and argues that all human efforts lead nowhere. He introduces the idea that everything is vapor. I like the idea that no one will remember just like "no one remembers the former generations" (Ecclesiastes 1:11). Though it can be scary, I feel like it also relieves a weight from our shoulders. I understand it as we have to worry less about waht others think, and focus on enjoying without worrying about being cringe.

    1:1-11

  • The Search of Meaning

    First, he looks for the value in wisdo, and he puts his "mind to study and to explore by wisdom all that is done under the heavens" (Ecclesiastes 1:13). However, he soon realizes that this is also meaningless and taht "the more knowledge, the more grief" (Ecclesiastes 1:18). After this, he forgets about wisdom and tries to find the meaning of life by indulging in "worldly pleasures" (The Book of Ecclesiastes Summary by Chapter 1:12). He concludes that folly is meaningles. Despite wisdom and folly being both meaningless and lead to the same fate, the teacher says "wisdom is better than folly" because it takes people out of darkness (Ecclesiastes 2:13).

    1:12-2:26

  • Time for Everything

    According to Henry (2026), to expect the world not cahnge in a changing worl "must en in dissapointment." As the teacher says, "There is a time for everything" (Ecclesiastes 3:1), and I think that is part of the beauty of life. We need to learn to appreciate the little things in life. When I was saying goodbye to my family in Mexico, I was really sad, and I thought there was nothing positive in that feeling. However, my grandma told me, think how fortunate you are to have people who make saying goodbye so hard, and how fortunate you are to have an opportunity that is worth saying goodbye. We need to accept and embrace every stage in our lives because life the the gift of God (Ecclesiastes 3:13).

    3:1-3:22

  • Wisdom and Suffering

    The teacher says that he has seen the opression under the sun and that the people that are dead "are happier than the living," (Ecclesiastes 4:2), but even better are those who "have never been born," because they have not "seen the evil that is done under the sun" (Ecclesiastes 4:3). This made me think of the phrase by a famous German philosopher, Friedrich Nietzsche (as cited in Kasi, 2024) "To live is to suffer, to survive is to find some meaning in the suffering." As the previous section mentioned, there is a time for everything, including suffering, but how we deal with that is what matters. This is connected to wisdom, which is also talked about in this section. The teacher says that wisdom is like a "shelter," that "preserves those who have it" (Ecclesiastes 7:12). To live is not the same as just existing; we must learn to find meaning form our suffering, but there is no "one-size-fits-all solution" to that problem (Kasi). The teacher, for example, finds his meaning in fearing God, and by "enjoying the few days of life God has given" (Ecclesiastes 5:18).

    4:1-7:14

  • Human Limits

    The teacher says that not even the wisest humans can comprehend what is going on in the earth, and “despite all their efforts to search it out, no one can discover its meaning” (Ecclesiastes 8:17). This is true, not only in religion but in science and life in general. These limitations do not come from a lack of will but from the “limitations of our senses, the complexity of the universe, and the limitations of language and communication” (The Limits of Human Understanding). This made me think of Fermat’s last theorem. For three centuries, people tried to find counterexamples, but they did not succeed, so it seemed that what Fermat said was true, but no one could prove it. It was not until a kid, Andrew Wiles, became obsessed with the problem and came up with some proof (Singh). I brought this up because, like this, there are several things that we know are true but cannot prove, and things that we can prove that we cannot fully understand. Like in one of Mark Miller’s classes, we talked about Gabriel’s horn, which is a revolutionized solid that has a finite volume of pi units, but an infinite surface area. I understood every step to get those results, but I do not understand them. These are some trivial examples to represent the complexity of the world we live in. The teacher says we will never understand, and only God does, and he recommends that we “obey the king’s command” (Ecclesiastes 8:2).

    7:15-9:18

  • Conclusion 10:1-12:14

    In chapter 10, the teacher explains that little things can ruin big accomplishments, and he gives examples like “dead flies give perfume a bad smell” (Ecclesiastes 10:1). He adds that it is important to use wisdom, respect, and good behavior. Chapter 11 is a reminder that life is full of uncertainties, and only one thing is clear: death. According to Kidner (2015), this is a reminder to enjoy the little things in life, because they do not last long, and that “they cannot be abused with impunity.” The teacher’s advice is to enjoy the gifts of God while we can and to do good under the Sun. He recommends the youth to enjoy those years of youth, but he warns that whatever you do, “God will bring you into judgment” (Ecclesiastes 11:9). He thinks that leaving a life without fearing God is a meaningless life (What Is Life About? The Conclusion of Ecclesiastes and a Most Beautiful Description of Aging). The teacher concludes in verse 13 of chapter 12 with the phrase “Fear God and keep his commandments, for this is the duty of all mankind.” This book is one of my favorites because it shows that the world is not perfect, but that is the beauty of it. Imagine a movie without a problem; it would be boring, and no one would watch it. I like that he invites us to enjoy the little things in life and look for our meaning. It also made me think about one of my favorite movies, which is life-changing: Trainspotting, and that made me think about what I want to do and who I am.

    10:1-12:14
